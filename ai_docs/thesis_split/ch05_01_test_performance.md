## **5.1 Forecasting Performance on the Test Dataset**

Figure 5.1 illustrates the comparison between the actual electrical load (blue solid line) and the forecasted load generated by the PSO-ELM model (red dashed line) over the first 300 hours of the test set.

Visually, the forecasted curve exhibits a tight alignment with the actual load curve, effectively capturing the periodic nature of the electricity demand. The model demonstrates strong capabilities in two specific aspects:

1. **Peak and Valley Tracking:** The PSO-ELM model successfully identifies the daily peak loads (occurring around 18:00 - 20:00) and the valley loads (occurring around 03:00 - 05:00) with minimal deviation. This indicates that the Cyclical Encoding features (sin/cos for hours) applied in the preprocessing stage effectively helped the network learn temporal patterns.

2. **Responsiveness:** The predicted signal shows no significant phase lag compared to the actual signal. This is a crucial improvement, as traditional

methods often produce a "shifted" forecast that lags behind real-time changes.

Quantitatively, the high accuracy is confirmed by the error metrics. Alongside the low MAPE and RMSE reported, the model achieved a **Mean Absolute Error (MAE)** of approximately 584,02 MW. This implies that, on average, the forecast deviates from the actual demand by only 828,89 MW, which is a negligible margin given the total system load range of 18,000 to 41,000 MW.
